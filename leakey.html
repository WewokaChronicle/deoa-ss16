Mary Leakey

Feb. 6, 1913 - Dec 9, 1996

Written By: Robert Billette

Growing Up

Mary Douglas Nicol was born on February 6, 1913 in London, England. Her father, Erskine Edward Nicol was a watercolor painter and traveler, and her mother cared for her while she was young, and helped educate Mary throughout her childhood. From a young age, Mary excelled at drawing and illustrating; a skill most likely acquired from her paternal lineage, and would later help her enter the field of anthropology (Bowman-Kruhm, 2005). The Nicol family often moved around, visiting numerous destinations in Italy, the United States, and Egypt. Her father painted watercolors on his travels across Europe, Africa, and the places aforementioned, and sold them in England. It was during these travels that Mary leaky developed a love for Egyptology and illustrating. When living in France she would often visit museums of Natural History, thus instilling an early knowledge of the natural world and history. During World War I, Mary’s family ceased travels and spent time in England. After the war the family resumed its cycle of journeys across Europe and a yearly return to London in which her father would sell the paintings her produced on his trips. She became fluent in French after her family moved to Cabrerets, France (Mary Leakey, n.d.). Mary’s mother had a history of various relatives who were antiquarians and archaeologists, known as the Freres. It may be assumed that through here family history, she was exposed to archaeology and eventually pursued a career in it. Her Father presumably influenced her in the field or art and illustration. Mary’s father died of cancer when she was only thirteen years old, and her mother retuned to London indefinitely. (Bowman-Kruhm, 2005)

Education

The majority of Mary’s education was informal and learned through practice and experience. She attended a Catholic Convent school for some time and failed to even maintain decent grades and compliance (Editors, n.d.). The nuns were agitated by here constant reluctance and behavior. Although fluent in French, she failed French language studies. The dialect she had learned while living in France was apparently not considered formal enough for the standard of the convent (Bowman-Kruhm, 2005). She was later expelled for refusing to recite poetry, and expelled from a second Catholic Convent school for causing an explosion in a chemistry laboratory. Her mother hired two tutors, but they were unsuccessful at educating Mary as well. Her mother contacted a professor at Oxford about a college education and was denied. Formal University admission was deemed nearly impossible given her academic record. Most of her learning came from attending lectures and classes at the London University and at the London Museum, although unregistered for classes. She attended lectures in archaeology, geology, history, and related subjects that suited her interests. He also learned much from the London museum, working and taking in every influx of information possible. After developing knowledge and making connections with archaeologists, she was able to find a career and move up in the field. After years of work and discoveries, Mary was awarded an honorary doctoral degree from Oxford University in 1951. Although not considered a “Doctor,” this award is nonetheless very prestigious and is indicative of a very educated individual, regardless of their formalities (Editors, n.d).


Early Career

While at the University and Museum, Mary made connections and she studied under Mortimer Wheeler, a British archaeologist. She was not yet fulfilling a position as an archaeologist, however she assisted him in analyzation of artifacts, but excluded from field excavations. Her involvement with archaeology took off when she started applying to work on a number of summer excavations. Her current mentor, Mortimer Wheeler, was the first to accept her after realizing her work ethic, passion, and potential (Mary Leakey, n.d.). She worked at St. Albans at the Roman site of Verulamium. She then worked for four years under Dorothy Liddell at the site of Hembury. She was trained and educated in field work, gaining invaluable experience, compensating for her failed attempts at formal education (Mary Leaky, n.d.). Her knowledge gained from experience cannot be even remotely taught in a classroom. The two types of education vary greatly; fortunately for her she had plenty of experience with both. A significant amount of her work included illustrating tools excavated by Liddell, and attracted the attention of notable archaeologist Gertrude Caton Thompson. She became an illustrator for Thompson’s book, The Desert Fayoum, and worked with her and Liddell concurrently (Mary Leakey, n.d.). These experience building, skill honing, jobs along with her inherent passion for anthropology, supplied her with the tools needed to be a successful archaeologist. Thus her career began.


Louis Leakey

While working for Gertrude Caton Thompson, Mary Nicol met Louis Leakey in 1933, who was seeking an illustrator for his current book. He invited her to Ethiopia for a field season to work with him, and Mary Accepted the offer (Editors, n.d.). A romance promptly ensued and their affair was eventually discovered by Louis’s wife Frida. After his divorce from Frida in 1936, Mary and Louis married, and she adopted the last name Leakey (Editors, n.d.). They bore three children, Johnathon, Richard, and Philip. Richard is the only son that persuaded archaeology, following the legacy of his parents. Louis was a great archaeologist. He not only cared a great deal about preservation of past humans, but he had an interest in, and was an advocate of primatology. He was involved in funding and organizing the project for Jane Goodall, Diane Fossey, and Birute Galdikas. The mission of this project was to better understand the life and habitats of primates. The three women observed and lived with Chimpanzees, Gorillas, and Orangutans, respectively ().


Advancing in the Field of Archaeology

Mary served her apprenticeship from 1930 to 1934 under Dorothy Liddell (Lents, 2015). With assistance and teaching from Liddell and various mentors, she gained indispensable knowledge about archaeology, including surveying, excavation, and analyzation of artifacts. As an illustrator, she became very attentive to physical details, a skill which would serve to be of utmost importance. After meeting Louis, the two worked together in Kenya at a number of Later Stone Age, Neolithic, and Iron Age archaeological sites. From the 1930’s to the 1950’s, the Leakeys’ prevailed in unearthing some of the most revolutionary discoveries in archaeology (Bowman-Kruhm, 2005). She blazed her own trail and was an innovator on the forefront of archaeological practice. The two together were creative yet methodical, unorthodox, and revolutionary to the field.


Work in Kenya

Mary made her first revolutionary discovery of her prosperous career in 1948, on Rusinga Island in Kenya. She uncovered Proconsul Africanus, later renamed Proconsul Hesoloni. It consisted of half a hominid skull with both the upper and lower jaws, and all of its teeth. The fossil revealed indication of a common ancestor for both apes and humans. The absence of a brow ridge suggests its closeness to humans, and its protruding lower jaw and canine teeth suggest relation to apes. However, this specimen is not an ancestor to either; it parallels the lineage of humans and apes, sharing a common ancestor (Smithsonian, n.d).

In 1959, Mary found hundreds of fragments of a hominid skull that she pieced together. The fossil, named Zinjanthropus boisei by Louis was later reclassified as Australopithecus boisei and now most commonly called Paranthropus boisei. The age of Zinjanthropus boisei reformed all current conjectures and speculations about the antiquity of hominids in Africa. It was dated to 1.75 million years ago and monumentally adjusted the existing timeline for human evolution. The species weighed around 100 pounds and stood about 4 and half feet tall, additionally exhibiting a slightly larger braincase than seen in earlier hominins (Smithsonian, n.d.).


Work at Olduvai Gorge in Tanzania

Merely one short year after the discovery of Zinjanthropus boisei, Mary and her husband Louis discovered another fossil recognized as revolutionary in the field of anthropology. This fossil was Homo habilis. This find marked a new era in human evolution, and allowed researchers to conclude it was the species that gave rise to more frequent tool use (Smithsonian, n.d.). This species had a combination of features different than those seen in Australopiths. A more distinguishing feature was its larger braincase, leading scientists to believe it is the species responsible for the increase in tool use across Africa, and the thousands of stone tools found in Olduvai Gorge during that time period. As a result of this, they are denoted the name Homo habilis, meaning “handy man.” (Smithsonian, n.d.)

Perhaps her most important discovery of her career was the 88 foot long stretch of fossilized footprints, perfectly preserved for 3.75 million years in wet volcanic ash. Just as concrete holds footprints of modern human footprints, the volcanic ash acted as natural conservation of ancient human footprints. These footprints remarkably enhance our understanding of hominoids. We can see that whatever animal left the tracks, it must have been walking on two legs, a remarkable transformation of species into bipedalism. It was originally believed that only two or three Australopithecus aforensis individuals were walking, but new technology and topographical scanning leads us to believe it was four individuals. The volcano erupted once again after the footprints were made, and preserved them until Leakey and her team made the discovery in 1978. The shape and impression made by the animals gives the indication that it was left by an early hominin, and the only early hominin found in similar strata and location during the time the footprints were made were Australopithecus Aforensis (Smithsonian, n.d.).


Major Contributions to Archaeology

Not only did Mary Leakey discover new species and fossils that are critical to explaining the past, but she has uncovered and classified massive amounts of artifacts ad stone tools. Stone tools are vital to understanding human behavior; we can infer what their actions and life may have been like by matching a tool or artifact to a specific behavior. By analyzing stone tool and figuring out how they are made, archaeologists can narrow down ideas about what life was like for premodern humans and hominins. Without Mary, some of these tools ay have never been found, and thus the knowledge that we have today would be nonexistent. She contributed to a better understanding of our past, and she may not have done it alone, but she was one key element to the discoveries that hold information. Archaeologists uncover finds that are left to much debate, and is a field marked by disputes of interpretation. Mary leaky was relentless in speculating wild and unique theories of evolution. Her embracement of persistence and motivation gave rise to dominating theories that were the product of her expertise. She contributed to changing the world of archaeology one small idea at a time (A Century of the Leakey, n.d.).

Olduvai Gorge was home to the rise of early humans, and thus the rise of stone tools. Mary Leakey discovered a plethora of stone tools associated with human use, and developed a system of classification for these “Oldowan tools.” The tem Oldowan is taken from the site found by Louis where the first stone tools of this kind were found. Mary’s system of classification included organizing the tools based on function. They were grouped into categories that included scrapers, choppers, and pounders. Although a very useful method and incredibly helpful to our understanding, there are some innately problematic issues that arise when we assume the function of a stone tool. Instead the system is reformed to classify the tools based on manufacture technique, which is easier to decipher and can tell us an equal, if not more information about the shifts in human intelligence. As humans became smarter, the manufacture technique for a tool may become more sophisticated, although the tool is used for similar functions as its predecessors, thus we can recognize an increase in species intelligence based on manufacture technique. Mary’s initial proposal and use of her classification method was extremely clever and a consistent and scientific approach to archaeology, and left a lasting impression in the field (Oldowan and Acheulean, n.d).


Legacy

In Great Briton, the Royal Mail created a postage stamp issue in (state year) in memorial of Mary Leakey.

As a celebration of the one-hundredth anniversary of her birthday, Google released a Google-doodle in dedication to Mary Leakey.

Mary and Louis’s son Richard followed in the interest of his parents. He showed aspirations of becoming an archaeologist and she trained him into a remarkable archaeologist today. His wife Meave and daughter Louise play a very important role in carrying on her legacy. She is an always will be one of the most revolutionary archaeologists in history (Bowman-Kruhm, 2005).
